Data Fabric

Sep 2020
Show Full Article Hide Full Article

Defining a data fabric architecture and comparing/contrasting it related approaches.

Traditionally, data lived in a Database Management System (DBMS) or in an unstructured format (e.g. shared-drive, other filesystems). As time passed, we now have both structured and unstructured data of various formats, located at various end-points.

Emerging from Data Management, Big Data, and associated Analytics, Data Fabric is a single environment allowing access to the data (you need) via a common toolset/framework irrespective of data location, residency, or format, while providing means to analyze and transform data as it moves between underlying system/data-layer. For an end-user, it is a single system that unifies the accessibility of data from various services, technologies into one platform. Data Fabric Architecture uses the fundamentals of software design and architecture to achieve such an objective.

Personally, it feels like it's a 1990s grid computing focused on distributed ETL involving a huge amount of heterogeneous data and real-time analytics.

Alternative approaches to Data Fabric rely on various data-warehousing technologies (pre-processed data for a particular purpose, gathered and flattened for analysis/reporting while providing query languages on top of it). Another alternative is Data Lakes (all data at one place, transformed as needed, used for analytics, various reporting, machine learning, etc.)

Pros and cons enterprises may find when implementing a data fabric architecture

The biggest advantage of having such a solution is the elimination of silos and improvement of the company's operations (due to data-driven decisions). Gathering data is easy and cheap these days. Also, the data is growing at an exponential pace.

There are many advantages of having a robust Data Fabric implementation.

  • By creating a unified data environment, you can improve collaboration between teams significantly. This is achieved by eliminating data silos.
  • The side-effect of such a process is reduced risk and cost, while concurrently achieving scalability as part of designing for growth.
  • Legacy Analytics: With the ability to have access to the data irrespective of formats or locations via centralized access control and APIs, the data can be mined for the right information, hence exposing its use in Analytics.
  • Edge Analytics: With the right architecture, as the streams of data are ingested, the proper alerting and edge analytics can be performed.
  • Interestingly, both the Legacy and Edge Analytics can be performed every time the data moves between the various endpoints in the mesh.

Data quality and associated governance continue to be the biggest challenge to Data Fabric. The next big challenge is the associate infrastructure (availability and durability)

  • Getting a functional Data Fabric is challenging as it involves exposing data endpoints that can talk (connect) to each other while complying with security, transformation, analytics, and various data-governance rules. Wrongly interpreted governance and wrongly implemented data transformation will result in wrong decisions (wrong data for data-driven decisions).

Success can be achieved if the Data Fabric approach is well thought through and the initiative is well funded.

Top use cases for data fabrics make the most sense and how do these work in practice?

At times, some of the data fabric implementations have been very scary as they have intruded extensively in eroding the privacy of citizens of many countries. In a non-controversial implementation, they help in data-driven decisions.

  • Data Fabric can be used at places that have heterogeneous disperse systems and data.
  • Take a look at social networking and the way the user's profile/activity/data is shared among heterogeneous applications. Your location, friends, groups you belong to, emails, shopping preferences, etc. although come from various data endpoints. The right implementation of Data Fabric will weave them together to provide various business/social/law-enforcement values.
  • Another example is trading/managing future energy needs via interplanetary energy grids. Energy generated in various ways can be priced based on "global" demands. It can be sold/traded, distributed/managed based on the demands of regions while interconnecting with the production and distribution of raw materials used for generating energy.
  • Below is another example of a highly secure SaaS organization operating in Hybrid Cloud
    • The security team would like to know when an individual enters, exits the building, and all activities performed by the individual. One can easily find out when an employee scans their access card on the main door and other secured locations within the premises (Physical Security). Once an employee enters a secured location, the employee logs into an authorized computer system. Login time, the number of unsuccessful login attempts are tracked. There is a lag between entering a secured location and successful login. The details of this lag-time are kept as time-series and an alert can be generated if the predictive lag of an individual employee exceeds the threshold (predictive analysis, simple AI for predictive behavior). Hence this required both the "data" points to be connected and have a real-time analysis performed within the fabric.
    • Activities based on the role are another area of interest.
      • The individual operates in a hybrid cloud, there are activities performed in provisioning the system, accessing the system, and operating on the same. If this was AWS, there would be a lot of cloud trail data generated, including the data generated while operating one or more on-prem ecosystems. Activities of access and others are logged. Logs from the application used during that time are also generated. Do note that all these are heterogeneous systems. To ensure a successful activity, this system must authenticate and authorize all activities. In cases like API calls, these authentication and authorization are done every time.
      • The individual has a Customer Service/Support role. This person has access to customer data and can make changes to it based on customer requests. These activity logs are generated in various systems. These activities may not be predictive as on some days, there are a lot of customer issues. However, accessing authorized customer data and making changes on their behalf are logged for compliance. One is also tracking the number of support tickets created and resolved, including the satisfaction (NPS) score around them.
      • Next add more persona (i) Salesperson responding to an RFI/RFQ after the initial request by the prospect (what is the duration of activity) and associated success of the sale, including the revenue amount. (ii) The accounting team making payment after "n" days of receiving the invoice, and a trend of payments received and payment made.
      • Now, with data fabric, I would like to weave them together. With the right algorithm in place, an organization will have real-time data of various sales activities, potential revenue realization, customer on-boarding time, and customer satisfaction, including associated alerts/escalation that are data-driven and measured.
